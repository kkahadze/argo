Mingrelian translator grammar compact reference

Use this as concise grammar guidance, not as lexicon evidence. Prefer exact
dictionary matches and parallel examples over any general rule below.

Writing and dialect
- Mingrelian is often written in Georgian Mkhedruli script. Do not assume a
  Mkhedruli string is Georgian; use the declared source language.
- Z-S often has ჷ, final -s may surface as -c after consonants, and plural -ep
  may surface as -em before narrative/dative. Senak often has -en.

Nouns and cases
- Base/nominative for consonant stems often ends in -i. After vowel-final stems,
  initial i of case suffixes drops.
- Cases: nominative -i/base, narrative -k, dative -s, genitive -iš/-š,
  allative -iša/-ša, ablative -iše/-še, instrumental -it/-t, designative
  -išo(t)/-šo(t), adverbial -o(t)/-t.
- Dative may be -c in Z-S after consonants. Genitive -š can weaken/delete.
- Emphatic vowels -i, -ჷ/-ə, -u may follow narrative, dative, genitive, and
  instrumental markers. Strip these cautiously when recognizing stems.
- Plural marker is -ep after the root; before narrative/dative it often becomes
  -en or -em. Root-final a may delete before plural.

Pronouns and demonstratives
- Personal pronouns: ma "I", si "you sg", mu "he/she/it"; čki "we", tkva
  "you pl", munepi "they".
- Genitive/possessive forms: čkimi "my", skani "your sg", muš "his/her/its",
  čkini "our", tkvani "your pl", munepiši "their".
- Demonstratives: tena/e/te "this"; tina/i/ti "that". They can act like
  third-person pronouns: "that one", "he/she/it", or contextual pronouns.
- Interrogatives: mi(n) "who", mu(y) "what", namu "which"; oblique forms add
  the regular case suffixes. Indefinites/negatives often build on these roots.

Noun phrase syntax
- Modifiers usually precede the noun and remain in base form. Following
  modifiers are declined and may agree in case/number.
- Genitive possessors/material/part-whole modifiers usually precede the head.
- Quantified nouns are commonly singular. Conjoined NPs use do "and"; the last
  conjunct must carry case.

Verb overview
- Mingrelian verbs encode person/number agreement, tense/aspect/mood
  ("screeves"), preverbs, and sometimes inversion. Nominal arguments may be
  omitted when recoverable from context, so translate pronouns from verb
  agreement only when needed in English/Georgian.
- Four broad classes: Class 1 transitive active; Class 2 intransitive inactive;
  Class 3 intransitive active; Class 4 stative/affective, often perception,
  feeling, possession, wanting, knowing.
- Present stems: Classes 1/3 usually consonant-final and take 3sg -s, 3pl -an.
  Classes 2/4 usually vowel-final and take 3sg -n, 3pl -nan. Word-final n often
  deletes unless followed by enclitic/suffix.
- Subject markers: 1sg often v-/b-/p-/p' by following sound; 2sg g-/r-/zero;
  1/2 plural use plural -t. Third person often has no prefix and uses suffixes.
- Object markers: 1st object m-/b-/p-/p'; 2nd object g-/r-/zero. Direct and
  indirect object markers are formally similar.

Preverbs, aspect, and polarity
- Verb prefix slots can include status/affirmative ko-, negative va-, direction
  and local preverbs, imperfectivizer, person marker, and preradical vowel.
- Common perfectivizing/future preverbs include še/ši, do/da/de/di, o, ga/go/ge,
  mi, mo, e, i. They may also add direction or lexical nuance.
- Simple preverbs include a-, o-, e-, ga-, go-, ge-, da-, do-, წა-, me-, mo-,
  še-. Complex/local elements include no-, to-, la-, ša-, c'o-, k'o-, da-.
- ko- is affirmative/perfective-supporting. va- negates finite verbs; negative
  pronouns are often based on mi-/mu- plus negative material.
- Vowels in stacked prefixes may delete; adjacent vowels may merge. A stripped
  form may hide an underlying preverb/person/preradical vowel sequence.

Screeves and case alignment
- Series I includes present/imperfect/subjunctive/conditionals and future-like
  forms. Series II is aorist-like. Series III/IV are evidential/perfect-like.
- Basic argument case pattern: in Series II, transitive final subjects take
  narrative -k and direct objects take nominative/base. In other series,
  subjects take nominative/base and direct/indirect objects often take dative.
- Inversion: Class 1/3 invert in Series III; Class 4 often invert in all series.
  The notional subject may appear as a dative/indirect object and control object
  agreement. Translate by semantic roles, not only surface case.

Translation heuristics
- For English/Georgian output, supply natural pronouns and tense even when
  Mingrelian omits arguments.
- For Mingrelian output, prefer plain translation over gloss lists. Preserve
  names and numbers. If dictionary evidence offers several senses, choose the
  sense fitting the full sentence and parallel examples.
- Treat figurative/secondary senses as lower priority unless context calls for them.
